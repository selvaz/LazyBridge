# Econometrics Overview for Quantitative Finance

## What Econometrics Is and Why It Matters

Econometrics is the application of statistical methods to economic and financial data for the purpose of testing theories, estimating relationships, and producing forecasts. It is not merely "statistics applied to finance" -- it addresses problems specific to economic data: non-experimental observations, time dependence, regime changes, simultaneity, and the fact that financial data is generated by adaptive agents whose behavior changes in response to observed patterns.

For quantitative finance specifically, econometrics provides the formal framework for:

- **Return prediction**: Estimating conditional mean models (ARIMA, regression) to forecast future returns, however modestly.
- **Risk measurement**: Estimating conditional variance models (GARCH) to quantify time-varying risk for VaR, portfolio allocation, and hedging.
- **Regime identification**: Detecting structural shifts in market behavior (Markov switching) that invalidate single-regime models.
- **Factor analysis**: Identifying systematic drivers of asset returns (OLS, panel regression) for portfolio construction and performance attribution.
- **Hypothesis testing**: Rigorously assessing whether an observed pattern (e.g., momentum, mean reversion) is statistically and economically significant or merely noise.

The stakes are high: a poorly specified model can produce forecasts that are worse than a naive benchmark, risk estimates that are systematically too low (leading to blown risk budgets), or regime classifications that lag reality by months.

## The Four Model Families in stat_runtime

### OLS (Ordinary Least Squares) Regression

OLS estimates linear relationships between a dependent variable and one or more explanatory variables by minimizing the sum of squared residuals. In finance, OLS is the workhorse for cross-sectional analysis (e.g., Fama-MacBeth regressions, factor models), simple trend estimation, and any setting where you need to quantify the linear association between variables. Use OLS when you have a clear dependent variable, plausible explanatory variables, and the relationship is approximately linear. OLS assumes that residuals are homoscedastic and serially uncorrelated -- assumptions that frequently fail in financial time series, making it the starting point rather than the endpoint for most analyses.

### ARIMA (Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average)

ARIMA models capture the temporal dependence structure in a single time series by combining autoregressive (AR) terms, differencing for non-stationarity (I), and moving average (MA) terms. The model exploits the fact that past values and past forecast errors carry information about future values. Use ARIMA when working with a single time series that exhibits autocorrelation in levels or returns -- for example, interest rate changes, inflation, or any series where the ACF or PACF shows significant structure. ARIMA is particularly well-suited for short-horizon forecasting (1-20 steps ahead) of series with identifiable serial dependence, and it naturally handles non-stationarity through its differencing parameter.

### GARCH (Generalized Autoregressive Conditional Heteroscedasticity)

GARCH models capture time-varying volatility by modeling the conditional variance as a function of past shocks and past variances. The core empirical regularity they exploit is volatility clustering: large return shocks tend to be followed by large shocks, and small shocks by small shocks. Use GARCH when your primary interest is in risk measurement -- estimating conditional volatility, computing Value-at-Risk, or producing volatility forecasts for option pricing and portfolio optimization. GARCH requires a stationary return series and is most informative for daily or weekly data where volatility clustering is pronounced. For equities, always consider asymmetric variants (GJR-GARCH, EGARCH) to capture the leverage effect.

### Markov Regime-Switching

Markov regime-switching models posit that the data-generating process alternates between a finite number of discrete states (regimes), each with its own parameters, with transitions governed by a Markov chain. Use regime-switching models when you believe the data exhibits distinct behavioral modes -- for example, bull and bear markets with different mean returns and volatilities. These models are particularly valuable for long-horizon asset allocation (adjusting portfolio weights based on the current regime), stress testing (estimating tail risk under the high-volatility regime), and for any analysis where a single-regime model produces residuals with clear evidence of structural instability.

## Decision Tree: Which Model Family to Start With

Given a financial dataset, use these decision rules to choose the initial model family:

### Step 1: What Is the Primary Question?

| Question | Start With |
|---|---|
| What drives cross-sectional variation in returns? | OLS regression |
| Can I forecast the next value(s) of this series? | ARIMA |
| What is the current risk level and how will it evolve? | GARCH |
| Has the market regime changed? What regime are we in now? | Markov switching |
| What is the factor exposure of this portfolio? | OLS regression |
| What is the Value-at-Risk for this position? | GARCH |
| Are there structural breaks in this relationship? | Markov switching |

### Step 2: What Are the Data Characteristics?

```
Is the series stationary?
├── Yes → Check for autocorrelation in levels
│   ├── Significant ACF → ARIMA (or AR model)
│   └── No significant ACF → Check squared returns
│       ├── Significant ACF in squared returns → GARCH
│       └── No ARCH effects → OLS or simple summary statistics
├── No → Apply differencing
│   ├── First difference is stationary → ARIMA with d=1
│   └── First difference still non-stationary → Check for trend, consider log-transform
└── Ambiguous (ADF and KPSS disagree) → See stationarity document; likely trend-stationary
```

### Step 3: Is a Single Regime Adequate?

After fitting the initial model:

- If residuals show volatility clustering that GARCH captures well, and diagnostics pass, stop.
- If parameters appear unstable across sub-periods, or if residual diagnostics reveal remaining structure despite a well-specified GARCH, consider Markov regime-switching.
- If the ACF of squared standardized residuals from GARCH still shows structure, consider Markov-switching GARCH (advanced).

### Combination Cases

Many real analyses combine families:

| Combination | Use Case |
|---|---|
| ARIMA + GARCH | Series with both mean autocorrelation and volatility clustering |
| OLS + GARCH residuals | Factor regression where residual risk is time-varying |
| ARIMA + Markov | Series with regime-dependent autocorrelation structure |
| Markov + GARCH within regimes | Regime-dependent volatility dynamics (advanced, data-intensive) |

## Key Concepts

### Stationarity

A time series is stationary if its statistical properties (mean, variance, autocorrelation structure) do not change over time. Stationarity is the foundational assumption for virtually all time-series econometrics. Asset prices are non-stationary (they trend upward over time); log returns are typically stationary. Every analysis begins by checking whether the series is stationary -- if not, it must be transformed (usually by differencing) before modeling.

**Why it matters**: Non-stationary data produces spurious regressions (Granger-Newbold, 1974), invalid test statistics, and misleading R-squared values. A regression of two independent random walks will show R-squared above 0.70 over 70% of the time.

### Autocorrelation

Autocorrelation measures the linear dependence between observations at different time lags. First-order autocorrelation (lag 1) is the correlation between r_t and r_{t-1}. The autocorrelation function (ACF) and partial autocorrelation function (PACF) are the primary tools for detecting temporal structure and selecting ARIMA orders.

**Why it matters**: Significant autocorrelation means past values carry information about future values, which ARIMA models exploit. In OLS residuals, autocorrelation violates the independence assumption, biasing standard errors downward and inflating t-statistics.

### Heteroscedasticity

Heteroscedasticity is non-constant variance of the error term. In time series, it manifests as volatility clustering -- periods of high volatility alternating with periods of low volatility. The ACF of squared returns detects this: significant autocorrelation in squared returns is the signature of conditional heteroscedasticity.

**Why it matters**: Heteroscedasticity makes OLS standard errors invalid (typically too small, producing false significance). More importantly, time-varying volatility is itself the primary object of interest for risk management, and GARCH models are designed to capture it.

### Regime Switching

Regime switching refers to discrete changes in the data-generating process -- shifts in mean, variance, or both that persist for extended periods. Bull and bear markets are the canonical financial example: mean returns are higher and volatility lower in bull markets, with the reverse in bear markets.

**Why it matters**: A model estimated over a mixed sample of bull and bear periods produces parameters that represent neither regime accurately. Regime-switching models separate these modes and provide probability estimates for the current regime, enabling regime-aware risk management and asset allocation.

## The Typical Analysis Pipeline

### 1. Data Preparation

- Obtain price or return data from a reliable source.
- Compute log returns: `r_t = ln(P_t / P_{t-1})`.
- Handle missing values (forward fill or interpolation; document the method).
- Compute summary statistics: mean, standard deviation, skewness, excess kurtosis, min, max.
- Note sample size and frequency. Is the sample large enough for the intended model?

### 2. Stationarity Check

- Run ADF test (null: unit root) and KPSS test (null: stationary).
- Interpret jointly: ADF rejects + KPSS does not reject = stationary. Both reject = likely trend-stationary. Neither rejects = inconclusive.
- If non-stationary, apply first differencing or log transformation. Re-test.

### 3. Exploratory Analysis

- Plot the ACF and PACF of the (stationary) series to identify autocorrelation structure.
- Plot the ACF of squared returns to check for ARCH effects.
- Run Engle's ARCH-LM test to formally test for conditional heteroscedasticity.
- Visually inspect the return series for obvious regime changes or structural breaks.

### 4. Model Selection

- Based on the decision tree above, choose the initial model family.
- Within that family, select the specific model order or specification using AIC/BIC and diagnostic checks.
- If AIC and BIC disagree, default to BIC for the more parsimonious model, unless the analysis is purely for forecasting (where AIC may be preferred).

### 5. Model Fitting

- Estimate parameters via maximum likelihood (or quasi-MLE for GARCH).
- Check convergence. If the optimizer did not converge, do not use the results.
- Verify that all parameters are statistically significant (p < 0.05). Insignificant parameters suggest over-parameterization.

### 6. Diagnostics

- Test residuals (or standardized residuals) for remaining autocorrelation (Ljung-Box test).
- Test squared residuals for remaining ARCH effects (ARCH-LM test, Ljung-Box on squares).
- Check residual normality (Jarque-Bera, QQ-plot) -- if non-normal, switch to fat-tailed distribution.
- For OLS: Durbin-Watson for autocorrelation, White's test for heteroscedasticity.
- For Markov: Regime Classification Measure (RCM < 0.30 indicates good separation).

### 7. Forecasting

- Produce point forecasts with confidence intervals.
- For GARCH: forecast conditional volatility; note that multi-step forecasts revert to unconditional volatility.
- For ARIMA: forecast conditional mean; note that confidence intervals widen with horizon.
- For Markov: report regime probabilities alongside forecasts.

### 8. Out-of-Sample Evaluation

- Reserve 20-30% of the sample for out-of-sample testing.
- Use expanding or rolling window evaluation.
- Compute out-of-sample metrics: RMSE, MAE, directional accuracy, out-of-sample R-squared.
- Compare against a naive benchmark (historical mean for returns, sample variance for volatility).

### 9. Reporting

- Follow the conventions in the report writing guide.
- Report all diagnostics, including failures.
- Include caveats and limitations.
- Distinguish statistical significance from economic significance.
- Never claim more precision than the model supports.

## When to Escalate Complexity

Start simple and add complexity only when diagnostics demand it:

```
OLS → residuals autocorrelated → ARIMA
ARIMA → residuals show volatility clustering → ARIMA-GARCH
GARCH → parameters unstable across sub-periods → Markov-switching GARCH
Any model → out-of-sample performance poor → reconsider specification or data
```

The parsimony principle applies throughout: a GARCH(1,1) that passes diagnostics is preferred over a GARCH(2,2) that marginally improves AIC. A two-regime Markov model with clear economic interpretation beats a three-regime model with ambiguous states. An AR(1) that forecasts well out of sample is preferred over an ARMA(3,2) that overfits.
